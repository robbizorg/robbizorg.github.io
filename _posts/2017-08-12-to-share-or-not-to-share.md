title: To Share or Not to Share
---

In order to satisfy the wants of a modern student, I have decided to start a blog. Who knows how often I'll update
it, if I do at all, but hey it's a good distraction for a lonely summer night.

Now, what does it mean to start a blog in this day and age? European writers many years now dead used to rely on
books and pamphlets to share their thoughts with the world. Today our great thinkers use blogs and a book for 
the longer attention spans. It begs the question, why do so many people, especially from my generation, share what they do
instead of being satisfied with merely living a private life?

It is expected that anyone aiming to reach the higher levels of society should start sharing some form of knowledge
whether they have something worth saying or not. Regardless of their intellect or knowledge, that ambitious 
writer is expected to share their thoughts, vapid they might be, with the rest of the world. There are many intuitive answers to this question. For example, one might note
that this sharing requirement is a healthy demand of an information society, therefore all productive members should be expected to
share their information with their colleagues and the world.

Although that's a tempting opinion, it seems to me as though it only captures part of the story. If sharing knowledge was 
really the important crux of this whole blogging world, why are people without knowledge expected to share at all? Why are 
undergraduate students, who are embarking on the formative years of their lives, told to start a blog and share their opinions
with the world when those opinions do not say that which has not already been said a thousand different times before? Our 
descendants will try to sift through the millions of blog posts to find the ones that added to the sum total of knowledge
on Earth and will be left wondering why so many uninformed youths took up the pen.

Now, this isn't to say that a certain form of knowledge sharing from people of our generation isn't valid or critical. Looking
at activism or other engaged forms of knowledge sharing, clearly young voices do matter in this regard. What I'm concerned with,
however, is the demand that's placed on people to talk on matters discussed many times before--the questions of happiness, loneliness, technical computer science tutorials on deep neural networks, etc. These topics have their experts, yet young people
feel compelled to write on them regardless.

I'm not trying to say that young people shouldn't write on things that interest them, it's probably good practice for the
future when they themselves are the experts, but why publish them now? Why go to the effort of creating thoughts and distributing
them for public consumption when those thoughts themselves really are not all that original, mind-blowing, or even eloquently 
phrased?

It's trite to say that social media and other techno-cultural factors are to blame for the desire to publish. In a society
where people assert originality and personality to their friends and themselves, it's natural that we share what we can to 
[indulge in our uniqueness, even if it's completely fabricated](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wdynfVNbJ5E). Facebook and other
forms of social media really are the quick result of this, where we can share any details of our lives with anyone whenever we 
have the slightest urge to, but more traditional forms (if they can be considered traditional at all) like writing extended personal essays or pamphlets have taken the form of the personal blog.

This is where part of the solution to the question lies. For the most part, we are social beings. Despite our individualistic tendencies, people rely on others for their upbringing, personality, skills, stories, and, well, mostly everything that makes them a human being. Numerating all of the contributions others make to an individual's life is beyond the scope of this short personal essay. Anyways, the desire to share ultimately has its roots in the human desire for social relationships. When I share information about myself with another person, that lays the foundations for trust and eventually true friendship. This can be seen as a normal state of affairs, which social media and the Internet has begun to exploit.

Social media platforms consist of two major users: content creators and lurkers. Lurkers are a vast majority of the users--those who will sit down for ten or more minutes looking over their feeds with the vague ennui of the 21st Century human. Content creators are those who leave comments, make posts, share/retweet/reblog the various scraps of knowledge. These roles are not completely separate, we are (mostly) lurkers and, when we feel like it, content creators.

Now here's the question that brings us one step closer to a solution to our problem: Who exists on the Internet, and, by extension, who exists in our digital society? Does a lurker exist? In the minds of the Internet and in the minds of the websites they frequent so often, it is hard to say that they do. Although they might have a username listed somewhere on a database, it's hardly more than a marker on a tombstone for someone from an age long forgotten. On the Internet, to exist is to be a content creator. Lurkers have no record of their existence outside of that database. Lurkers are even unaware of the existence of other lurkers. 

You might be thinking, but we all know that other people use the Internet. We know that there are many people who read and are influenced by content online. Of course, I respond, these people do exist in the physical sense. They take up space in whatever we consider the real world to be nowadays, but, unlike content creators, they do not play an active role in the marketplace of ideas.

Content creators, on the other hand, are the ones who actually exist in this digital age. Not only do they imprint their mind on the digital landscape, but they additionally have the affirmation of other creators that they do indeed exist. One creator writes an article, another responds. In the digital world, this polemic marks the existence of the two creators while millions of lurkers look on. 


A thought does not exist until it is actually out in the open. Therefore, we do not exist until we are out in the open. [I share, therefore I am.](http://www.mit.edu/~sturkle/) Looking at a network of individuals, the one who shares is the one who is noticed while, bright though they may be, the private competitor wallows away in anonymity for having the gall to think alone.
